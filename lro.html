Lunar Reconnaissance

Orbiter

The Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter (LRO), launched in 2009, maps the Moon in high detail to study its surface, identify landing sites, and detect water ice deposits for future missions.

LRO (Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter) was the first U.S. mission to the Moon in over 10 years. LRO created a 3D map of the Moon, as part of a program to identify future landing sites and resources - including deposits of water ice shadowed in polar craters. LRO continues to orbit the Moon.

Lunar Orbiter Laser Altimeter

One of the most crucial instruments aboard the Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter is the Lunar Orbiter Laser Altimeter (LOLA), which has played a key role in creating an incredibly detailed 3D map of the Moon's surface. By firing laser pulses at the lunar terrain and measuring the time it takes for them to bounce back, LOLA can determine elevation with extreme accuracy, helping to identify potential landing sites and navigate the rugged lunar landscape. This data has been essential for planning future missions, as it reveals hazards like steep slopes and deep craters while also highlighting regions of interest, such as permanently shadowed areas that may contain water ice. Beyond its practical applications for human exploration, LOLA's high-resolution topographic maps have deepened our understanding of the Moon's geological history, shedding light on the processes that shaped its surface over billions of years.
